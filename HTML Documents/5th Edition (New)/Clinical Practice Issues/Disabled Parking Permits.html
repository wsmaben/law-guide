Disabled Parking Permits

Why is my patient asking me for a prescription when applying for disabled parking privileges?

As of July 1, 2015, individuals will need a prescription to apply for Washington's special parking program for persons with disabilities. This means applicants will first need to get a licensed physician, physician assistant, or registered nurse practitioner to determine if the applicant can qualify for disabled parking privileges.1

What if I don't have access to a prescription pad?

Providers who do not have access to a prescription pad, or who do not have prescriptive authority, may issues an authorization on their office's official letterhead or by electronic means for the patient to use during the application process.2

What are the potential penalties to me as a physician/physician assistant related to assisting a patient with a disabled parking permit?

A health care practitioner who knowingly provides false information on the patient's application is guilty of a gross misdemeanor. The penalty is up to 364 days in jail and a fine of up to $5,000 or both. In addition, the health care practitioner may be subject to sanctions under chapter 18.130 RCW, the Uniform Disciplinary Act.3

What conditions qualify my patient for special parking privileges?

  • In order to qualify for disabled parking privileges, your patient must have a disability that meets at least one of the following criteria:4 The patient can't walk 200 feet without stopping to rest;
  • The patient's ability to walk is severely limited due to an arthritic, neurological, or orthopedic condition;
  • The patient is so severely disabled that he/she can't walk without the use of or assistance from a brace, cane, another person, prosthetic device, wheelchair, or other assistive device;
  • The patient uses portable oxygen;
  • The patient is restricted by lung disease to such an extent that forced expiratory respiratory volume when measured by spirometry is less than 1 liter/second or the arterial oxygen tension is less than 60 mm/hg on room air at rest;
  • The patient is impaired by cardiovascular disease or cardiac condition to the extent that his/her functional limitations are classified as Class III or IV under standards accepted by the American Heart Association;
  • The patient has an eye condition of a progressive nature that may lead to blindness;
  • The patient has acute sensitivity to light associated with a form of porphyria that would significantly benefit from a decrease in exposure to light or
  • The patient has a disability resulting from an acute sensitivity to automobile emissions which limits or impairs his/her ability to walk. The patient's physician, physician assistant, or advanced registered nurse practitioner must document that the patient's disability is comparable in severity to the others listed above.

Where can I find more information?

Visit the Washington Department of Licensing website at www.dol.wa.gov.

If you have questions about disabled parking privileges, you may contact the Department's customer service.
